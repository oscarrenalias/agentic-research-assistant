Title: Small Modular Nuclear Reactors (SMRs) and the Social Impact of Cheap, Commoditized Clean Energy

Objective:
Draft a research-backed LinkedIn post exploring whether SMRs could make nuclear energy cheap enough to become a widely available commodity, and what that could mean for society.

Audience:
Tech leaders, policy professionals, climate/energy founders, and curious general professionals.

Rough angle:
- We may be entering an era where "energy abundance" becomes a strategic advantage.
- If SMRs reduce cost and deployment time, energy could shift from a limiting constraint to a growth enabler.
- The post should be optimistic but realistic: include both upside and key risks.

Core points to explore:
- Why SMRs are different from traditional gigawatt-scale nuclear plants:
  - Smaller units, factory-style manufacturing potential, modular deployment, lower upfront capital per unit.
- Cost dynamics:
  - Could serial manufacturing and standardized designs bring down $/MWh over time?
  - What are the biggest cost drivers today (licensing, first-of-a-kind costs, financing, fuel cycle, waste handling)?
- Grid and reliability impact:
  - Firm low-carbon power for data centers, hospitals, manufacturing hubs, and electrified transport.
  - Complement to intermittent renewables by reducing storage/backup pressure.
- Climate and decarbonization:
  - Potential role in replacing coal/gas in hard-to-abate grids.
  - Tradeoff vs scaling renewables + storage only.
- Geopolitics and industrial policy:
  - Countries with faster SMR commercialization may gain manufacturing and AI/compute advantages.
  - Energy independence and resilience implications.
- Societal effects if clean power becomes materially cheaper:
  - Lower energy poverty, cheaper desalination, more affordable housing/construction inputs, stronger digital infrastructure.
  - Potential acceleration in AI, biotech, and advanced manufacturing due to lower operating costs.
- Risks and constraints (must include):
  - Safety, waste management, public acceptance, regulatory delays, cost overruns, supply chain bottlenecks, decommissioning obligations.

Potential sources to investigate:
- International Energy Agency (IEA) reports on nuclear and electricity.
- IAEA resources on SMRs and deployment status.
- U.S. DOE and national lab publications (e.g., INL) on advanced reactors and cost assumptions.
- OECD Nuclear Energy Agency (NEA) analyses.
- Peer-reviewed studies on levelized cost of electricity (LCOE), learning curves, and system-level decarbonization pathways.
- Public filings and project updates from leading SMR developers (cross-check claims).

Data points worth finding:
- Current and projected LCOE ranges for SMRs vs gas, coal, utility-scale solar/wind + storage.
- Typical construction timelines and capacity factors.
- Historical learning rates in nuclear vs other energy technologies.
- Estimated impact of cheaper electricity on GDP/productivity in energy-intensive sectors.

Questions to answer explicitly:
1) Under what conditions could SMRs become truly cost-competitive at scale?
2) What would need to happen in regulation and financing for commoditization to occur?
3) If cheap nuclear power becomes real by the 2030s, which sectors and communities benefit first?
4) What is the strongest skeptical argument, and how should proponents respond?

Tone and style constraints:
- Evidence-first, no hype.
- Short paragraphs, concrete claims, and clear source attribution.
- End with a practical takeaway for business and policy leaders.

Draft output preference:
- 1 LinkedIn post draft (~900-1300 characters).
- 1 longer version (~400-700 words) with explicit references list.
